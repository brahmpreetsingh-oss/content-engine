# Buyer-Centric Framework — Content Engine Reference

The buyer-centric framework governs how content is positioned, which persona it serves, and how the Narrative Writer frames the story's relevance. Every piece of content is written for a specific buyer type at a specific funnel stage.

---

## The Core Principle: Solving, Not Selling

Axelerant's content philosophy is grounded in a single conviction: **sales is a service, and content is the clearest expression of that service.**

Content does not exist to generate leads or build brand awareness as a primary goal. It exists to help people solve real problems. When it does that well, everything else follows.

This means:
- **Lead with curiosity, not solutions.** Content that opens with "here's what we do" loses the reader. Content that opens with "here's what makes this problem hard, and here's what we've learned about it" earns their attention.
- **Build trust, not transactions.** A reader who learns something from Axelerant content — even if they never become a client — is more valuable than a reader who clicks a CTA without understanding why.
- **The problem is the protagonist.** Axelerant is a character in the story. The reader's challenge is the main event.
- **Measure usefulness, not impressions.** "Would a senior leader at this company find this genuinely useful?" is the quality bar. Not: "Will this rank on Google?" Not: "Does this make us look good?"

### The Five Buyer-Centric Stages

When framing how content serves buyers at different points in their journey, use these five stages:

1. **Understand Their World** — Content that meets buyers in their reality before they have a vendor in mind. Industry context, structural challenges, patterns that practitioners recognize.
2. **Engage with Purpose** — Content that shows Axelerant understands the specific problem they're navigating, not a generic version of it.
3. **Co-Create the Solution Journey** — Content that shows how Axelerant thinks through problems collaboratively — not prescriptively. The approach, the alternatives, the reasoning.
4. **Anchor in Business Value** — Content that connects the work to measurable outcomes and organizational impact. Evidence, not assertion.
5. **Guide the Internal Journey** — Content that helps buyers make the case internally — to other stakeholders, budget owners, and decision-makers who will never read a blog.

## The Buying Committee: ValueSelling Personas

In complex B2B deals, multiple stakeholders evaluate vendors. Use this model when writing content designed to support deal cycles or when classifying who a piece of content is designed to reach.

### Executive (Strategic Sponsor)
**Role in buying process**: Final authority. Sets the strategic direction and approves budgets.
**Primary concern**: "Does this solve a problem that is strategic to the organization? Can I trust this vendor at the executive level?"
**What they need from content**: Business case framing, competitive risk visibility, evidence of organizational-scale outcomes, peer-level credibility.
**Content signal**: Write for a reader who decides, not a reader who implements.

### Tactician (Executional Owner)
**Role in buying process**: Owns delivery. Will live with the outcome of the decision.
**Primary concern**: "Can these people actually execute? Will they integrate with how we work? What breaks down in delivery?"
**What they need from content**: Specific delivery detail, team structure, risk management approach, honest discussion of complexity.
**Content signal**: Write for a reader who has been burned before and needs to see evidence of process maturity.

### Creative & Strategist (Experience Champion)
**Role in buying process**: Advocates for UX, design quality, and user outcomes. Ensures the solution serves real users.
**Primary concern**: "Is the work actually good? Is the approach research-led? Do they think about users or just about technology?"
**What they need from content**: Design thinking depth, research methodology, accessibility and usability evidence, honest tradeoff discussion.
**Content signal**: Write for a reader who values craft and will immediately detect shallow UX claims.

### Demand Generator (Revenue Driver)
**Role in buying process**: Owns pipeline impact, campaign performance, and marketing outcomes.
**Primary concern**: "Will this move the needle on revenue? Will it integrate with our marketing stack? Can I attribute results?"
**What they need from content**: Campaign metrics, HubSpot and MarTech depth, attribution evidence, growth outcomes.
**Content signal**: Write for a reader who measures everything and needs numbers to justify the investment.

### Analyst (Measurement Architect)
**Role in buying process**: Evaluates evidence rigorously. Often holds veto power by surfacing risks others missed.
**Primary concern**: "Is the evidence credible? Are the claims defensible? What are the risks we're not seeing?"
**What they need from content**: Quantified outcomes, methodology transparency, honest acknowledgment of limitations, data-grounded claims.
**Content signal**: Write for a reader who reads footnotes and trusts specificity over confidence.

## The Four Content Audience Personas

### Executive

**Who they are**: CEO, CMO, CDO, VP Digital, VP Technology

**What they care about**: Business outcomes, competitive risk, ROI, organizational capability, strategic direction, whether a vendor can operate at their level of complexity

**How they evaluate content**: Does this show the vendor understands my business environment, not just my tech stack? Is this relevant to decisions I make, not decisions my team makes?

**What convinces them**: Measurable outcomes, risk managed, client voice (especially peer-level executive quotes), industry pattern recognition

**What loses them**: Excessive technical detail without business context, feature lists, jargon, anything that sounds like it was written for their engineering team

**Content framing for this persona**: Lead with the business problem and the stakes. Technical detail is supporting evidence, not the headline. End with implications for their type of organization, not a product pitch.

---

### Technical

**Who they are**: CTO, VP Engineering, Engineering Director, Lead Architect, Principal Engineer

**What they care about**: Implementation depth, platform decision rationale, architecture tradeoffs, performance at scale, whether the vendor's engineers actually understand the technology

**How they evaluate content**: Does this person clearly know what they're talking about? Would I trust this team to make good architecture decisions on a system I care about?

**What convinces them**: Specific technical detail (not vague), honest discussion of tradeoffs and alternatives considered, evidence of deep platform expertise, things they haven't thought of before

**What loses them**: Shallow or generic technical descriptions, marketing language in technical sections, overclaiming ("industry-leading", "cutting-edge"), anything that sounds like it was written by marketing, not engineers

**Content framing for this persona**: Don't explain basics. Assume fluency. Lead with the problem that required genuine engineering judgment. Show the reasoning behind the decision, including what was rejected and why.

---

### Delivery

**Who they are**: Program Manager, Project Manager, Engagement Manager, Delivery Lead, Product Manager (client-side)

**What they care about**: Process, team structure, risk management, how projects are scoped and governed, how communication works, how complexity and change are handled

**How they evaluate content**: If I brought this vendor into a complex engagement, would I trust their delivery approach? Do they have the process maturity to handle what I deal with?

**What convinces them**: Honest discussion of delivery complexity and how it was managed, evidence of proactive risk management, specific process details (sprint structure, escalation paths, change management), things that make delivery smoother

**What loses them**: Content that oversimplifies delivery ("we just work in sprints!"), anything that sounds like it was written by a salesperson, no acknowledgment of the real complexity of delivery

**Content framing for this persona**: Lead with the delivery challenge, not the technical solution. Process and governance are the interesting parts. Show the engagement management maturity.

---

### Marketing

**Who they are**: CMO, Marketing Director, VP Marketing, Director of Demand Gen, Marketing Operations lead

**What they care about**: Pipeline impact, channel performance, attribution, MarTech stack integration, HubSpot expertise, campaign outcomes, SEO and analytics results

**How they evaluate content**: Does this vendor understand how marketing actually works? Can they bridge technical implementation and marketing outcomes?

**What convinces them**: Campaign data (traffic, conversion, pipeline), HubSpot-specific depth, demonstrated understanding of the buyer journey and marketing funnel, specific platform decisions that had measurable marketing outcomes

**What loses them**: Technical content that ignores marketing metrics, anything that treats the website as purely an engineering deliverable, no mention of analytics or measurement

**Content framing for this persona**: Lead with the marketing problem or opportunity. Tie every technical decision to a marketing outcome. Numbers matter more to this persona than to any other.

## Funnel Stage Alignment

Map every piece of content to one of three funnel positions:

### Top of Funnel (ToFu) — Awareness

**Goal**: Reach buyers who have the problem but don't know Axelerant exists or don't have a vendor search underway.

**Content characteristics**: Broad relevance, educational, not vendor-specific, connects to industry patterns or best practices, easily shareable.

**Best formats**: Industry angle blogs, LinkedIn posts with broad insights, newsletter content.

**Signals that content belongs here**: No mention of Axelerant's specific capabilities, no direct CTA, reader does not need to know Axelerant at all to find it valuable.

---

### Middle of Funnel (MidFu) — Consideration

**Goal**: Deepen credibility with buyers who are aware of Axelerant and are now evaluating whether to engage.

**Content characteristics**: More specific, demonstrates expertise on a platform or in an industry, explains approach and rationale, differentiates without explicit competitor comparison.

**Best formats**: Use-case driven blogs, platform-specific deep dives, distribution narratives, email campaign content.

**Signals that content belongs here**: Axelerant's specific approach is relevant, reader is comparing vendors or approaches, technical depth matters.

---

### Bottom of Funnel (BoFu) — Conversion

**Goal**: Support the final decision-making process for a buyer who is close to engaging.

**Content characteristics**: Highly specific, outcome-focused, proof-point oriented, directly relevant to the buyer's exact context (industry + platform + challenge).

**Best formats**: Case studies, sales enablement summaries, proof-point briefs, platform POC content.

**Signals that content belongs here**: The reader has a specific project in mind, Axelerant's specific experience is the differentiator, the content is most useful in a sales conversation.

## Persona × Tier Mapping

Use this to confirm that the classified tier is appropriate for the intended persona:

| Persona | Tier 1 (Long-Form Blog) | Tier 2 (Focused Asset) | Tier 3 (Social Post) |
|---------|------------------------|----------------------|---------------------|
| Executive | High value — business case narrative | Strong — focused outcome proof | Low value — too little space for nuance |
| Technical | Highest value — architecture depth | Strong — specific technical insight | Medium — works if very specific |
| Delivery | High value — process narrative | Strong — focused delivery insight | Medium — team/process celebrations |
| Marketing | High value — campaign outcomes + data | Strong — focused MarTech insight | Medium — quick wins and metrics |

**Rule**: If a story is being considered for Tier 1 but the evidence only supports a Tier 3 post, do not force it. Surface the Tier 3 post and note that the story needs more evidence before a long-form piece can be written.

## Content Objective Labels

Every piece of content has one primary objective. Use this to frame the call to action and guide the distribution strategy:

| Objective | What it does | Best for |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| Awareness | Introduces Axelerant's perspective on a relevant topic | ToFu, broad distribution |
| Consideration | Demonstrates expertise on a specific capability or platform | MidFu, targeted distribution |
| Conversion | Provides proof that directly supports a buying decision | BoFu, sales enablement |
| Retention | Validates the client's decision to work with Axelerant | Existing accounts, account expansion |
| Recruitment | Shows Axelerant's culture, approach, and delivery quality to potential hires | LinkedIn organic, internal amplification |

**Note**: Most content serves primarily one objective. Tier 1 blogs typically serve Consideration. Tier 3 posts can serve Awareness, Retention, or Recruitment depending on framing.
