# Engagement Themes — Axelerant Content Engine

The five engagement themes are the primary lens through which Axelerant categorizes delivery work. Every story maps to exactly one theme. The theme determines which aspects of the work to foreground in the narrative and which audiences are most likely to find it credible and useful.

---

## The Five Themes

### Strategy

**What it covers**: High-level planning, goal-setting, frameworks, long-term vision, digital roadmaps, transformation planning

**When to assign it**: The primary value in the story is the thinking — the approach to framing the problem, the framework used to scope the solution, or the strategic decision that shaped everything downstream.

**Typical evidence**: Discovery call transcripts where the problem scope was defined, SOW documents that articulate the strategic rationale, retrospectives where the strategy decision was validated.

**Audience fit**: Executive persona (CEO, CMO, CDO, VP Digital) — cares about business outcomes, direction, ROI.

**Content angle fit**: Industry angle (connecting the strategic lesson to broader industry patterns); also works as use-case driven when the strategy is tied to specific implementation context.

**Example**: A healthcare association's move from a legacy CMS to a headless architecture wasn't a technical decision — it was a digital self-determination decision that unlocked the ability to own their content across every channel they would ever add. That's a Strategy story.

---

### Design

**What it covers**: User experience decisions, design systems, user research inputs, interface rationale, accessibility improvements, design process, creative direction

**When to assign it**: The primary value in the story is a design or UX decision — how the team understood the user's need, what they learned from research, or why a particular design approach was chosen over alternatives.

**Typical evidence**: UX research synthesis documents, design decision records, Granola calls where user testing results were discussed, client feedback on design direction.

**Audience fit**: Technical persona (for design system architecture), Executive persona (for accessibility outcomes or conversion impact), Marketing persona (for UX improvements tied to campaign performance).

**Content angle fit**: Use-case driven (the design decision and its rationale); use-case with platform focus (Drupal/Acquia's design system capabilities); industry (UX patterns specific to a vertical like higher education or associations).

**Example**: A university redesigned their program search experience based on moderated user research that revealed students were searching by career outcome, not by subject area. The structural content change that followed doubled course inquiry rates. That's a Design story.

---

### Build

**What it covers**: Technical implementation, coding decisions, development approaches, architecture choices, integrations, technical constraints and how they were navigated, engineering-specific problem-solving

**When to assign it**: The primary value in the story is the technical execution — how the system was built, what architectural choices were made, how specific technical constraints were overcome.

**Typical evidence**: Architecture decision records (ADRs), technical deep-dive calls in Granola, Slack threads where engineering debates were resolved, performance benchmarks before/after.

**Audience fit**: Technical persona (CTO, Engineering Director, Architect) — cares deeply about implementation depth, platform decisions, tradeoffs.

**Content angle fit**: Use-case driven (implementation logic and architecture); use-case with platform focus (Drupal/HubSpot/AWS/Acquia as central to the solution).

**Example**: Migrating a healthcare association's content model to a structured, platform-agnostic format required building a custom migration pipeline that handled 14 content types with zero content loss. That's a Build story.

---

### Grow

**What it covers**: Scaling decisions, performance optimization, SEO improvements, conversion improvements, analytics implementations, post-launch iteration, A/B testing and growth experiments, metrics-driven improvements

**When to assign it**: The primary value in the story is the improvement over time — what was measured, what was changed, and what got better as a result of continuous iteration.

**Typical evidence**: Analytics reports showing before/after comparisons, retrospectives focused on post-launch performance, HubSpot notes about client satisfaction with improving metrics.

**Audience fit**: Marketing persona (CMO, Marketing Director, Demand Gen) — cares about pipeline, channels, attribution; also relevant to Technical persona for performance work.

**Content angle fit**: Industry angle (growth patterns specific to a sector); use-case driven (specific optimization approach tied to implementation).

**Example**: Three months after launching a redesigned product discovery system, the client's add-to-cart rate improved 34% — but only after a post-launch iteration cycle that addressed a critical content categorization flaw discovered in analytics. That's a Grow story.

---

### Delivery

**What it covers**: Project delivery success, engagement management, team structure and coordination, risk identification and management, client communication approach, delivery process innovation, handling complexity or ambiguity

**When to assign it**: The primary value in the story is how the work was managed and delivered — the process, the team dynamics, the risks navigated, the client relationship managed. The "how we delivered" is more interesting than the "what we built."

**Typical evidence**: Retrospectives discussing what went well and what was difficult, Granola calls where delivery challenges were discussed, HubSpot notes capturing client feedback on the working relationship.

**Audience fit**: Delivery persona (PM, Program Manager, Delivery Lead) — cares about process, team structure, risk management; also relevant to Executive persona buying delivery capability.

**Content angle fit**: Use-case driven (delivery approach tied to specific context); industry (delivery patterns specific to a sector, e.g., how to manage associations with complex stakeholder approval chains).

**Example**: A 14-month migration delivered on time and within budget despite a scope expansion in Month 9 — because the engagement model included a formal change management protocol that had been built into the original SOW. That's a Delivery story.

## Theme Assignment Rules

1. **One theme per story** — assign the most prominent theme, not all themes the story touches
2. **The theme describes the story's primary value**, not all the work involved — a Build story that involved good delivery is still a Build story
3. **When in doubt between Build and Delivery**, ask: "Would a CTO find this primarily interesting for the technical architecture, or for how the project was managed?" The answer determines the theme.
4. **Strategy stories are rarest** — most engagements are execution stories, not strategy stories; only assign Strategy when the thinking genuinely takes precedence over the doing
5. **Grow stories require measurable improvement** — if there are no numbers (or at least directional evidence of improvement), it is probably a Build or Design story

## Theme × Tier Compatibility

Not all theme/tier combinations are equally natural:

| Theme | Tier 1 Fit | Tier 2 Fit | Tier 3 Fit |
|-------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Strategy | ✅ Strong (industry angle) | ✅ Strong (focused insight) | ⚠️ Rare |
| Design | ✅ Strong (UX decision + outcome) | ✅ Strong | ✅ Good (team/process moment) |
| Build | ✅ Strongest fit (technical depth) | ✅ Strong | ✅ Good (engineering moment) |
| Grow | ✅ Strong (before/after metrics) | ✅ Strong | ✅ Good (quick win) |
| Delivery | ✅ Strong (complex delivery story) | ✅ Strong | ✅ Good (team milestone) |

All themes can support all tiers — but the weakest combination is Strategy + Tier 3. Strategy insights are hard to express meaningfully in a short social post.
