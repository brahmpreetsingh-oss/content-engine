# Social Post Understanding — Reference

Deep reference for constructing LinkedIn posts that earn attention, deliver genuine value, and serve the right business objective.

## The Fundamental Problem With Most B2B Social Posts

Most B2B LinkedIn posts fail for one of three reasons:

1. **The hook is generic** — "Excited to share our latest blog!" tells the reader nothing specific and earns no click.
2. **The body withholds the insight** — treating the post as a teaser rather than the content itself destroys trust and trains followers to stop engaging.
3. **The format doesn't match the message** — a process breakdown formatted as a text wall, or an emotional story packaged as an infographic, loses the impact of both.

The fix is a disciplined three-layer framework: scroll-stop hook → value-adding body → right creative format.

---

## Layer 1: The Scroll-Stop Hook

LinkedIn shows the first 2–3 lines before the "see more" cutoff. These lines function as the headline, the first impression, and the ad. If they don't earn engagement, the rest of the post doesn't matter.

### What Makes a Hook Work

A hook works when it creates cognitive tension — the reader encounters a statement that contradicts their assumption, confirms their suspicion, or promises a specific answer to a question they actually have.

**Specificity is the primary driver of scroll-stop.** Generic statements create no tension. Specific claims, numbers, or named outcomes create instant credibility and curiosity.

Compare:
- ❌ "We recently completed a complex migration project with impressive results."
- ✅ "A 14-month migration timeline. We cut it to 9 months without adding headcount. Here's the architectural decision that made the difference."

### Hook Archetypes

**The Outcome Lead**: Start with the measurable result, then explain how.
> "3x organic traffic improvement in 6 months — and it started with a CMS decision, not an SEO campaign."

**The Contrarian Claim**: State something that challenges conventional wisdom in the reader's domain.
> "Most Drupal migrations fail for reasons that have nothing to do with Drupal."

**The Precise Observation**: Make a specific observation that signals deep domain expertise.
> "Every enterprise migration we've seen fail had one thing in common: the content model was treated as a Phase 2 problem."

**The Direct Question**: Ask the question your reader is privately wrestling with.
> "Why do digital transformation initiatives always cost twice as much and take three times as long?"

**The Declarative Moment**: Start mid-story with a scene-setting detail.
> "Six weeks before launch, the client's board asked us to add multilingual support. We'd never planned for it. Here's what we did."

### What Kills a Hook

- Starting with the company name: "Axelerant recently..."
- Starting with "I'm excited to..." or "Proud to share..."
- Starting with "In today's digital landscape..."
- Starting with a feature list
- Using the word "journey"

## Layer 2: The Value-Adding Body

The body of the post should leave the reader with something real — an insight, a framework, a decision rationale — that they would have had to read a full article to get otherwise.

This sounds counterintuitive. If you give away the insight, why would they read the blog?

The answer: readers who got something genuine from the post are far more likely to engage with the full article than readers who were teased. The post earns trust. The blog deepens the relationship.

### What Belongs in the Body

- The core insight in plain language
- The "why behind the what" — not just what happened, but the reasoning
- Enough specificity that a practitioner in the field recognizes it as real
- A short, honest framing of the challenge that set the context

### What Doesn't Belong

- Long setup before the payoff ("We were working with a client who needed...")
- Lists of 7 things (3–4 max, and only if they genuinely warrant enumeration)
- Jargon that requires the reader to already be an expert
- Phrases like "we leveraged our expertise" or "our world-class team"

### Length and Rhythm

- 150–300 words is the sweet spot for most Tier 1 distribution posts
- Short paragraphs (2–4 sentences) with line breaks improve readability on mobile
- Avoid walls of text — LinkedIn's mobile interface punishes them

## Layer 3: Format Selection

The visual format is not decoration — it determines whether the content gets consumed at all. Different message types need different containers.

### Video (60–90 seconds)

**Use for**: Emotional stories, team moments, client relationships, culture content, founder/leader perspectives

**Why it works**: Video builds human connection at a speed text can't match. Emotion — pride, surprise, satisfaction — lands through face, voice, and presence. A story about a team overcoming a delivery crisis is 10x more impactful as a 90-second video than as a carousel.

**Production note**: Captions are mandatory. 80%+ of LinkedIn video is watched with sound off.

**Do not use for**: Technical architecture, data-heavy comparisons, process walkthroughs with many steps

### Carousel (5–8 Slides)

**Use for**: Process breakdowns, methodology walkthroughs, step-by-step guides, frameworks, decision trees

**Why it works**: The swipe mechanic turns passive consumption into active engagement. Readers control the pace. Carousels naturally structure content into digestible units and generate significantly higher engagement than static posts.

**Slide structure**:
- Slide 1: Hook (same rules as the text hook — specific claim or question)
- Slides 2–7: One idea per slide. One visual or icon. Minimal text.
- Final slide: Key takeaway or soft CTA

**Do not use for**: Simple announcements, emotional stories, single-idea posts

### Infographic / Single Image

**Use for**: Technical architecture diagrams, before/after comparisons, data visualizations, platform comparisons, structured frameworks with visual hierarchy

**Why it works**: Dense information that requires visual layout to parse — topology diagrams, decision trees, multi-variable comparisons — is legitimately better as an image than as a text post.

**Do not use for**: Everything else. Infographics are overused as default B2B content; they work only when the information genuinely requires visual layout.

### Long-Form Text Post (No Visual)

**Use for**: Perspective pieces, opinions, lessons learned, reflective content, expert commentary

**Why it works**: A text-only post is a strong signal of genuine expertise. It says: "I don't need visuals — my thinking is the content." Long-form text posts consistently outperform image posts when the writer has real credibility and the topic is genuinely interesting to the audience.

**Optimal length**: 600–900 words. Short enough to read in full. Long enough to develop an argument.

**Do not use for**: Announcements, launches, milestone celebrations

### Single Static Image

**Use for**: Launch announcements, milestone celebrations, simple brand moments, quotes

**Why it works**: Visual-first moments — a launch, a team win, a new partnership — benefit from a strong image that creates emotional recognition instantly.

**Do not use for**: Anything requiring explanation, technical content, process descriptions

## Platform-Specific Best Practices

### LinkedIn Organic

- Post Tuesday–Thursday, 8–10am local time (consistent with audience timezone)
- First comment from the author with the blog link outperforms link-in-post for reach
- Engage with comments within the first 60 minutes — algorithm rewards this heavily
- Tag relevant people by name only when their contribution is genuinely being credited, not for reach inflation

### LinkedIn Sponsored (Campaign Snippet)

- Headline: 70 characters max, outcome-first
- Body: 150 characters max
- CTA button: "Learn More" or "Read Now" outperforms "Download" for blog content
- A/B test the hook against a different angle — never run a single ad creative for more than 2 weeks

### Email / Newsletter

- Subject line follows the same hook rules as LinkedIn — specific claim beats generic announcement
- Blurb is not a summary — it is a standalone value proposition for the article
- Link text should describe what the reader will get, not just "click here": "See the architecture breakdown →"

## The Content-Format Decision Tree

```
Is the core message primarily emotional?
  → Yes → Video
  → No → Continue

Is it a multi-step process or framework?
  → Yes → Carousel
  → No → Continue

Does the insight require visual comparison or data layout?
  → Yes → Infographic / Single Image
  → No → Continue

Is this a milestone, launch, or celebration?
  → Yes → Single Image + Strong Hook
  → No → Long-Form Text Post
```

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | Why It Fails | Fix |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| Using the same hashtag set on every post | Audiences overlap; algorithm deprioritizes repetitive tagging | Rotate hashtags by topic and audience |
| Writing the post as a blog summary | Summary ≠ value; readers feel cheated | Give the insight directly; use the blog for depth |
| Over-tagging people | Looks like reach-baiting; damages credibility | Only tag contributors, not influencers |
| Posting at inconsistent times | Algorithm rewards consistency | Build a posting schedule and stick to it |
| Never using video | Misses the highest-engagement format | One video post per month minimum on team/delivery stories |
| Emoji overuse | Signals low-quality content to professional audiences | Zero emojis or one maximum, only when the post is explicitly conversational |
